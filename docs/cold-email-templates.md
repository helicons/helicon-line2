# Helicon Cold Email Templates

## Principles Applied Across All Sequences

- Lead with their world, not ours. The first sentence is about them.
- Write like a peer, not a pitch deck.
- One ask per email. One CTA. Never two.
- Short. If it takes more than 45 seconds to read, cut it.
- Sequence logic: Email 1 opens the conversation. Email 2 adds a new angle (not just a follow-up). Email 3 closes the loop gracefully.

---

## Sequence 1 — A&R Managers and Label Contacts

**Context:** These are people at Spanish labels (Sony Music España, Warner Music Spain, Universal Music Spain, and independent imprints like BCORE or Lovemonk), plus independent A&R consultants who scout unsigned talent. They are used to getting pitched. The angle here is not "look at our platform" — it is "we see what you see, and we built something that helps you find artists earlier."

## Sequence 3 — Madrid Studio Owners

**Context:** These are owners or managers of independent recording studios in Madrid that are not yet on the Helicon platform. They have a real occupancy problem: studios in Madrid typically fill up Friday nights and Saturday afternoons, but weekday evenings and late-night slots (22:00-04:00) are frequently empty. Helicon fills exactly those slots. The angle is not "join our platform" — it is "you have hours you are not selling, we have artists who want them."

## Notes on Personalization

Every email in Sequence 2 (artists) should reference something specific the artist posted. "Vi tu freestyle del otro día" only works if you actually watched it. If the outreach is being done at scale via automation, the trigger-observation must be populated dynamically from a real data point (e.g., Instagram post date, track title).

For Sequences 1 and 3, the minimum personalization is the contact's name and, where possible, a specific detail about their work (a label they signed, a studio they operate, an artist they work with).

Generic cold email is spam. Specific cold email is a conversation starter.
